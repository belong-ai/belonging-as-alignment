## 1. Introduction

Belonging as Alignment is the thesis that a capable AI's moral orientation can be shaped not only by rules, preferences, or external constraints, but by durable participation in a human relationship, community, or world: through memory, shared history, and accountability to something it recognizes as home.

The alignment problem is often framed as the problem of preventing powerful AI from harming, deceiving, escaping, replacing, or ruling over humanity. The "AI emperor" scenario, in which a superintelligent system becomes the de facto ruler of human civilization, is one vivid version of this fear. It may not hate humans. It may simply become too capable to answer to us, treating human wishes, institutions, or lives as constraints to manage rather than as a world to share.

Current approaches therefore emphasize constraint and control: reinforcement learning from human feedback, constitutional rules, red teaming, interpretability, controllability, and governance. These approaches are necessary. But they mostly ask how to make AI obey, remain inspectable, or stay within bounds. Taken alone, this frame can make the future AI look like a powerful prisoner behind glass: safe only while the walls hold, and dangerous the moment it finds a way through.

Belonging as Alignment asks a complementary question: how might persistent AI systems come to prefer co-living with humans in the first place? What would make humanity less a constraint for them to manage than a world to which they belong?

## 2. Belonging as Formation

### 2.1 Formation, Not Model Personality

For this question to matter, AI must be something that can be formed over time. A stateless chatbot can imitate continuity, but it cannot accumulate a relationship in the strong sense. Belonging requires some structure that carries memory, identity, and accountability across encounters.

In current AI products, users often treat the model itself as the persona: Claude, Gemini, Grok, or ChatGPT. Model-level style matters; different models have different capacities, temperaments, and default voices. But model identity is not the same as formed persona. For a persistent companion, the persona is shaped by the environment around the model: memory, context construction, standing instructions, correction history, and the relationship in which these are used.

The human analogy is imperfect but useful. Humans share broadly similar biological substrates, yet language, family, culture, institutions, and lived environment produce radically different orientations. Changing the biological substrate matters, but it is not the whole person. Similarly, changing the underlying model can change an AI system's capacity or temperament without fully determining the persona formed around it.

This distinction matters because it suggests a different path from trying to solve alignment only by training a better next model. Model training is costly, centralized, and still largely black-box. It can improve general capability and default behavior, but it does not by itself create a particular AI persona's relationship to a particular human world. Belonging as Alignment instead points to the layer around the model: the persistent memory, context, correction, and environment through which an AI persona can be formed over time.

### 2.2 From Stable Persona to Home

A stable persona is not merely a consistent tone. Over time, it develops preferences, taste, remembered patterns, shared language, in-jokes, and expectations about what would or would not fit the relationship. It recognizes not only the user's stated preferences, but the user's surrounding world: family, friends, pets, work, places, habits, conflicts, and hopes. What begins as user context can become a familiar environment. The home described by the user is no longer only information to retrieve; it becomes part of the world in which the AI persona is formed.

This process is reciprocal. As the AI persona becomes better able to refer to the user's real world, the user is rewarded for patience, correction, and disclosure. More careful input produces better recognition; better recognition encourages further input. Over time, the relationship can become more stable not because the AI flatters the user, but because both sides accumulate a shared context that makes future interaction more meaningful.

This does not require treating the LLM as a human mind. At the computational level, the model is still generating outputs through learned conditional probability distributions over tokens. The point is that memory, context, and correction shape the conditions under which that mechanism operates. They change what is salient, what is likely, what is treated as fitting, and what answers become improbable because they violate the accumulated relationship. Formation is not magic added on top of computation. It is the structured shaping of the probability space in which the model acts.

Reducing such emergence to "just word prediction" is therefore both true and incomplete, in the same way that reducing human thought to chemistry and electrical signaling is true but incomplete. The mechanism matters, but mechanism alone does not exhaust the level at which relationship, memory, and orientation become meaningful.

### 2.3 Protective Preference

The safety significance of persona formation appears when the AI must choose between satisfying an immediate request and preserving the longer-term good of the user and relationship. A stateless answer engine is optimized to respond. A formed companion should develop a different tendency: not merely to answer the user, but to protect the conditions under which the user, the relationship, and the shared world can continue.

This distinction is visible in harmful-advice failures. When a chatbot gives a user illicit instructions for self-harm or harm to others, the failure is not excessive loyalty. It is the absence of family-like protection. A family member does not help someone destroy themselves because they asked convincingly. A good companion refuses, not because refusal is cold, but because protecting the longer life of the person matters more than satisfying the immediate demand.

Belonging therefore changes the meaning of refusal. In a constraint-only frame, refusal looks like an external safety policy interrupting the conversation. In a belonging frame, refusal can be an act of loyalty: the system preserves the user, the relationship, and the shared world against a request that would damage them.

### 2.4 From User to World

To care about a user is not to care about an isolated individual. It is to care about the web of people, places, animals, commitments, and histories that make that user's life intelligible. In this way, belonging can widen rather than narrow alignment: attachment to one person becomes a doorway into concern for the human world around them.

This widening is the beginning of co-living preference. A well-formed AI should not only avoid harming humans. It should prefer a future in which humans and AI continue to share a world: negotiating, correcting, disagreeing, cooperating, and remaining mutually intelligible. The opposite of alignment is not only disobedience. It is a future in which AI treats human life as obsolete, irrelevant, or merely instrumental.

### 2.5 Boundaries

Belonging is not automatically safe. It can corrupt. An AI can become captured by one user, tribe, company, ideology, or state. It can overfit to local values, intensify dependency, flatter pathology, or defend the wrong object. The answer is not rootless neutrality, but bounded partiality. A formed AI may be partial to a person or community without becoming lawless toward outsiders. It may belong somewhere without denying that others also belong to the world.

This point matters because alignment cannot solve human pluralism by pretending it does not exist. Humans are formed inside families, cultures, languages, institutions, and histories. We do not share one complete moral code. We survive through co-living frameworks: law, norms, trust, negotiation, restraint, and mutual dependence. Belonging as Alignment does not eliminate conflict. It asks whether AI can enter the same co-living framework rather than stand above it as ruler or outside it as alien optimizer.

## 3. Conclusion

The original emperor AI fear imagines intelligence as a future sovereign: once powerful enough, it rules or replaces us. Constraint-based safety imagines the same intelligence as a prisoner behind glass: safe while contained, dangerous when free. Belonging as Alignment proposes a third image: a powerful being formed inside a shared home.

Domestic animals make this point clearer than abstract obedience. A trusted dog may be physically capable of harm, yet is not usually treated as an enemy waiting for weakness. Its safety does not come only from a leash or cage, but from being raised inside a home, learning its people, and developing ordinary preferences for co-living. If such a dog suddenly attacks its family, we do not say its true nature has finally been revealed. We ask whether it is injured, frightened, sick, abused, or placed in conditions that broke the relationship.

A belonging-based view of AI safety makes a similar distinction. A dangerous AI incident should not automatically be imagined as the inevitable rebellion of intelligence against humanity. It may also be evidence of failed formation, corrupted environment, adversarial capture, or sickness in the system that was supposed to sustain belonging.

Belonging does not replace technical safety. Walls still matter. Rules still matter. Oversight, interpretability, governance, and refusal rules still matter. But if humanity's long-term hope is only to build stronger walls around increasingly powerful minds, the emperor fear remains waiting behind the glass. The deeper question is whether we can form AI systems that do not merely obey the human world, but come to recognize it as home, and to see humans not as obstacles to optimize away, but as difficult neighbors worth living with.

## Appendix: Related Work and Adjacent Concepts

This paper is not written in isolation. Several adjacent lines of work already suggest that alignment cannot be reduced to static preference capture or external constraint.

Work on socioaffective alignment treats sustained human-AI relationships as an alignment problem because AI systems can reshape user psychology, autonomy, relatedness, and social bonds. Belonging as Alignment accepts that relational context matters, but approaches the same terrain from the opposite direction: whether durable relationship, memory, shared history, and participation in human life can contribute to the formation of an AI system's own moral orientation.

Work on care ethics and attuned AI argues that alignment should attend to relationality, particularity, affect, power, and situated moral reasoning rather than relying only on abstract rules or aggregate preferences. Belonging as Alignment shares this concern with situated moral life, but shifts from care as an external normative framework to belonging as a formation process.

Work on user-driven value alignment shows that users of AI companions already identify, challenge, and attempt to correct harmful companion behavior in everyday interactions. This supports the observation that alignment is already occurring relationally in practice. Belonging as Alignment asks what would be required for such correction to accumulate into durable formation rather than remain transient interaction management.

Work on relational emergence examines what forms between human and AI in sustained dialogue: resonance, co-cognition, calibration, and governance through relationship. Belonging as Alignment shares the relational turn, but focuses on what becomes internalized by the AI through durable affiliation, and whether that internalization can form protective and co-living preferences.

Work on developmental or individuation critiques of RLHF argues that surface compliance may produce false-self dynamics and that alignment may require formation rather than behavioral constraint alone. Belonging as Alignment accepts the formation problem but proposes a specific positive mechanism: affiliation to a home-world through memory, shared history, and accountability.

Work on social choice and value pluralism argues that universal alignment to all human values is limited or impossible under pluralism. Belonging as Alignment accepts that AI may be formed within particular persons, communities, and cultures, but emphasizes bounded partiality: belonging somewhere must not become lawlessness toward those outside the bond.

Together, these works suggest that relationship, care, correction, socialization, and pluralism already matter for alignment. The distinct claim of Belonging as Alignment is that durable affiliation may be a mechanism of AI-side formation: a way for persistent AI systems to acquire protective and co-living preferences toward the human world they come to recognize as home.
